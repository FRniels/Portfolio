# Reflection

## Korthagen Reflection Model

### Action

This year, PXL organised a visit to PVS, a company active in large-scale professional audio applications. My relationship with PVS actually predated the organised visit — I had independently applied for a position there roughly half a year earlier which was cancelled at the last minute because they eventually did not want to invest at that point of time. This made visiting them again a mixed feelings event. However I chose to attend regardless, as seeing the inside of the company firsthand still seemed worthwhile.

The visit offered a look into the day-to-day operations and technical environment of PVS. Following the group visit, PVS reached out to me again and we had an additional meeting to discuss potential opportunities. During these conversations I was clear about my primary interest in FPGA development, while also indicating openness to embedded software roles — provided there would be room to eventually introduce FPGA work into my responsibilities.

### Looking Back

The visit itself was genuinely informative and left a positive impression of the company. The people were approachable, the environment was interesting, and the context of large-scale audio infrastructure gave plenty to think about technically.

The follow-up meeting added a personal dimension that made the experience feel more meaningful than a standard company tour. That said, the silence that followed — now roughly three months on — has made the outcome fairly clear. Reading between the lines, the FPGA angle I was hoping for is unlikely to materialise there, at least not in the near term. That is a reasonable outcome for both sides; they know their roadmap and I know my priorities.

What made it slightly more pointed is that PVS has expressed ambitions in video and security — areas where FPGA development is not just relevant but arguably a natural fit. Whether that potential ever translates into the kind of role I was describing is their call to make, and I hold no frustration over it.

### Awareness of Essential Aspects

This experience reinforced something important: aligning with a company is about more than a shared interest in the same industry. It requires a match in technical direction, timing, and the willingness on both sides to invest in something new. PVS is a solid company delivering reliable solutions at scale, and that is genuinely respectable. But large-scale audio deployment and high-end audio engineering are different worlds, and my background as a trained musician makes that distinction feel quite tangible.

The visit also highlighted the value of pursuing opportunities independently — not waiting for a school-organised event to make first contact. Having already applied before the PXL visit gave me a different level of engagement during the tour, which made it a richer experience than it might otherwise have been.

### Creating Alternatives

If a similar situation arose, I would communicate my technical expectations even more explicitly during the early stages of contact — not to the point of being inflexible, but clearly enough that both sides can assess the fit before investing further time. Leaving key interests implicit, even with a polite nod that they would "keep it in mind," introduces ambiguity that does not serve either party well in the long run.

From a broader job search perspective, this reinforced that companies with active FPGA or hardware development roadmaps — particularly in video, security, or signal processing — are where my energy is best spent. Audio is a genuine personal interest, but the technical core of the work needs to match.

### Trial

Going forward, I will continue reaching out to companies independently rather than waiting for organised visits, as direct initiative tends to produce more meaningful engagement. I will also be more deliberate about evaluating whether a company's technical trajectory — not just their current work — aligns with the kind of development I want to be doing. A company heading in the right direction is often a better fit than one whose current work is adjacent but whose future is unclear.

## PXL X-Factor

* **(Em)passion:** I had already independently applied to PVS before the school visit was arranged, demonstrating genuine proactive interest rather than simply attending an organised event.
* **Entrepreneurial:** Taking the initiative to apply early and following through on the subsequent meeting — on my own terms and with a clear sense of what I was looking for — reflects an entrepreneurial approach to career development.
* **Innovative:** My interest in introducing FPGA development into an embedded software role at PVS reflects a forward-looking perspective, particularly given the company's stated ambitions in video and security — domains where hardware acceleration is highly relevant.
* **(Inter)nationality:** PVS operates in large-scale professional audio for events and installations, giving the visit an international industry context and exposure to a professional sector with a broad geographic reach.
* **Multi-perspective:** As both an electronics student and a trained musician, I was able to approach the visit from two distinct angles — evaluating both the technical environment and the audio domain — which gave me a more nuanced view of where the company sits in the broader landscape.

## Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

### SDG 4 – Quality Education

The PVS visit contributed to SDG 4 by providing a real-world engineering environment to observe and engage with outside of the academic setting. The combination of an organised group visit and an independent follow-up meeting created a layered learning experience that went beyond what a standard company tour would typically offer.

### SDG 9 – Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure

PVS's work in large-scale audio infrastructure and their future ambitions in video and security align directly with SDG 9. The visit offered insight into how engineering companies develop and deploy complex integrated systems at scale — and how hardware and software decisions shape the reliability and scalability of that infrastructure. It also sparked reflection on where emerging technologies like FPGA-based processing could play a role in evolving that infrastructure further.

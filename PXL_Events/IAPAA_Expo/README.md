# Reflection

## Korthagen Reflection Model

### Action

During the first year of the Electronics-ICT bachelor, our class travelled to Amsterdam to visit the IAPAA expo, an international trade fair organised by one of our teachers. The visit was intended as an opportunity to experience a professional industry event in an international setting.

The expo itself was centred around the entertainment and attractions industry. Exhibitors included companies active in luna park attractions, inflatable leisure equipment, large-scale stage construction such as festival setups, and similar entertainment infrastructure. As a group, we walked the exhibition floor, observed the various stands, and had the opportunity to interact with fellow students outside of the regular classroom environment.

### Looking Back

Going into the expo, my expectation was something closer to an embedded systems or electronics-focused trade fair — the kind of event where you encounter new hardware platforms, innovative PCB designs, or cutting-edge industrial technology. The IAPAA expo operates in a very different space, and that gap between expectation and reality was noticeable.

That said, the trip was not without its merits. Travelling internationally with classmates created a relaxed and social atmosphere that is hard to replicate in a classroom setting. It was a good opportunity to get to know fellow students better, and the logistics of organising and attending a professional event abroad was itself a mild learning experience.

### Awareness of Essential Aspects

The most honest takeaway from this visit is the importance of setting accurate expectations before attending any professional event. Had I known the expo was specifically oriented toward the entertainment attractions sector, I could have approached it with a different mindset — looking for transferable insights around large-scale system integration, crowd-safe engineering, or mechanical design, rather than expecting embedded electronics content.

The visit also made me more aware of the sheer breadth of industries that rely on engineering and electronics in non-obvious ways. Entertainment infrastructure involves structural engineering, power distribution, and control systems, even if those elements are not the visible focus of an expo floor.

### Creating Alternatives

If a similar trip were planned again, I would suggest researching the expo's exhibitor list and industry focus in advance, so students can identify relevant content and set realistic expectations. Supplementing the visit with a targeted debrief — discussing what transferable engineering concepts could be observed, even in an unfamiliar sector — would help extract more learning value from the experience.

Alternatively, combining the international trip with a visit to a second, more technically aligned event or company tour would make the overall experience more rewarding from an electronics and embedded systems perspective.

### Trial

In the future, I will take more time to research the scope and focus of professional events before attending. Going in with calibrated expectations — and a deliberate mindset for finding relevant takeaways even in unexpected contexts — makes any visit more valuable than arriving with assumptions that may not match reality.

## PXL X-Factor

* **(Em)passion:** While the content of the expo did not align with my technical interests, I engaged with the visit openly and made the most of the opportunity to spend time with classmates in an international environment.
* **Entrepreneurial:** Attending a professional trade fair, even outside my direct field, contributed to a broader awareness of how engineering and large-scale infrastructure intersect with industries I would not normally encounter.
* **Innovative:** The visit prompted me to think about how to extract relevant insights from events that fall outside one's primary area of interest — a skill that is useful in any professional context.
* **(Inter)nationality:** Travelling to Amsterdam for an international industry event was a practical experience of navigating a professional setting abroad, contributing to personal and interpersonal development within the group.
* **Multi-perspective:** The expo offered a view into a sector I had little prior exposure to, and while it differed from my expectations, it broadened my awareness of the variety of industries that engineering touches.

## Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

### SDG 4 – Quality Education

This visit contributed to SDG 4 by providing students with an experience outside the traditional classroom — attending a professional, internationally oriented trade event. Even where the technical content did not directly align with the Electronics-ICT curriculum, the experience of participating in a professional environment abroad has educational value in itself.

### SDG 11 – Sustainable Cities and Communities

The IAPAA expo, focused on large-scale entertainment infrastructure and public attractions, touches on SDG 11 in its connection to the design and operation of public spaces and leisure environments. Observing how industry professionals approach the construction of large public installations offered a peripheral but relevant perspective on the engineering of spaces that serve communities at scale.
